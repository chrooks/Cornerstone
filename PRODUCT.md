# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

NBA stat nerds who treat roster building like a strategy game. People who know what PnR efficiency means, who argue about skill tiers, who want to test "what if I built around prime Hakeem?" They come for the depth and stay because it feels like play.

Secondary audience: friends, colleagues, and recruiters seeing this as a portfolio piece. The landing page needs to impress both crowds without dumbing down for either.

## North Star

Cornerstone is the engine for the barbershop argument. Every basketball nerd has had the conversation: "$15 to build a starting five." "Five different eras of LeBron, which years do you pick?" "Best possible roster around prime Hakeem?" These debates happen at cookouts, in group chats, over 2K sessions, and in Instagram comment sections.

Cornerstone is the digital means to enact those hypotheticals. Build the roster. See how it scores. Compare it against other people working with the same rules. The product turns "I think my team would win" into something you can actually test.

The inspiration is those social media posts where you get $15 to assemble a lineup of players at varying tiers. Cornerstone is what happens when that concept gets a real evaluation engine behind it.

## Product Purpose

Cornerstone is an NBA skill evaluation and roster building platform. It rates players across a 21-skill taxonomy using a hybrid of statistical thresholds and AI assessment, then lets users build salary-capped rosters around legendary cornerstones.

The landing page exists to hook visitors in 5 seconds: show them the system is sharp, the analysis is real, and building a roster is going to be fun.

## Brand Personality

**Precise, kinetic, playful.**

- Precise: every number matters, every rating is earned, the system is rigorous
- Kinetic: energy and motion, not static dashboards; this is a game, not a spreadsheet
- Playful: building rosters is fun, discovering skill profiles is satisfying, the UI rewards exploration

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS landing pages (gradient blob hero, "Built for teams who...", floating mockup screenshots)
- ESPN and mainstream sports media (busy, ad-saturated, corporate, lowest-common-denominator)
- Default shadcn/Tailwind templates (gray-on-white, safe, forgettable, indistinguishable from every other Next.js project)
- Overly minimalist developer portfolios (too quiet, no energy, nothing to interact with)
- Dashboard-first layouts that feel like work instead of play

## Design Principles

1. **Data as decoration**: stats, skill tiers, and the 21-skill taxonomy ARE the visual interest. No decorative gradients or abstract shapes needed when real data is more compelling.
2. **Earned density**: show depth without overwhelming. Reveal complexity progressively. Baseball Savant's analytical sharpness, but with more breathing room.
3. **Game energy, not dashboard energy**: this is fun, not work. Every interaction should feel like play, not data entry.
4. **Sharp geometry**: rectangles, grids, precise alignments. Clean edges over soft rounded shapes. Statistical, not friendly-corporate.
5. **Show, don't tell**: demonstrate what Cornerstone does with real data and interaction, not marketing copy describing features.

## References

- **JJ Redick — "Old Man and the Three" Build Perfect Team Around X Legend series** (origin inspiration): The original spark for Cornerstone. Redick and guests pick a Legend, then build the ideal supporting cast around their specific skill set, weaknesses, era, and identity. This is the actual barbershop argument made into a recurring format. The Cornerstone Player ($54M anchor) and the supporting cap mechanic are direct descendants of this concept.
  - https://youtu.be/BSoMNDk3_xw
  - https://youtu.be/uq8Fplg813k
- **Pokemon Showdown** (play.pokemonshowdown.com): Inspiration for the product structure. Metagames/tiers = RuleSets. Each format defines a different game with different constraints. Team builder under format rules → battle → compare. The RuleSet selection screen should feel like picking a Showdown tier.
- **NBA 2K MyBuilder/MyLab**: UX nomenclature inspiration. "Build" as a configuration in progress. "Lab" as the space where you experiment. Large audience overlap expected.
- **Basketball GM** (basketball-gm.com): Inspiration for tone, product depth, and the "real engine running underneath, no fluff" feel. BBGM's changelog cadence, format-first defaults, and willingness to expose simulation/evaluation internals to a knowledgeable audience are direct touchstones. The Cornerstone changelog (issue #18) calls out BBGM by name.
- **Baseball Savant** (baseballsavant.mlb.com): Visual design inspiration. Sharp rectangles, clean data presentation, statistical confidence. Less density, more air.
